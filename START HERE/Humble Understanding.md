After working through hours of instructional content, studying the MITRE ATT&CK framework, and asking what felt like thousands of questions, there came a point where everything started to click. With guidance from an experienced SOC analyst and a growing understanding of how real-world incidents unfold, I finally reached the point where I was ready to step in and investigate on my own.

At this stage in my journey, as I approach the completion of my cybersecurity degree, Microsoft Sentinel no longer feels unfamiliar—it feels like the next step. The concepts, workflows, and patterns I see within the platform connect directly to what I’ve been building over time.

Throughout this process, I’ve developed a strong appreciation for digging into data. Whether it’s analyzing logs in a SIEM or working with tools like Wireshark and Autopsy, I take pride in my ability to interpret metadata and follow activity at a deeper level. I’ve often found myself thinking about how powerful it would be to combine these perspectives even further, as understanding both network traffic and system-level artifacts adds another layer to any investigation.

This moment—transitioning from guided learning to independent analysis—is where I began to truly see myself not just as a student, but as someone capable of thinking and operating like a SOC analyst.
