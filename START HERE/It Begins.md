To build on that foundation, I moved into Microsoft’s official Sentinel/Azure lab hosted on GitHub. My goal was simple: become more comfortable navigating the platform and understanding where to find the information that actually matters during an investigation.

What I didn’t realize at the time was that I had stepped into the middle of a transition period—Microsoft shifting focus from Sentinel into Defender. Because of that, parts of the lab were slightly outdated, and not every step could be followed exactly as written. Instead of being a setback, this turned into one of the most valuable parts of the experience.

I was forced to adapt—navigating unfamiliar menus, figuring out where features had been moved, and learning how the workflow had evolved. It stopped being about following instructions and became about understanding the platform.

During this process, I leaned heavily on Josh and several of his SOC coworkers. At one point, I found myself in a Discord call with multiple analysts actively working in the field I’m pursuing. That moment was almost surreal. Being able to ask questions in real time, troubleshoot alongside them, and see how they approached problems gave me insight that no lab or module could replicate.

It was challenging, but it was also one of the most rewarding experiences I’ve had so far—because it felt real.


From there, the experience shifted in a way that made everything feel real. Josh began building custom incident scenarios within his own lab and sharing them with me to investigate independently. I can’t overstate how valuable that was—having someone with real-world experience take the time to create scenarios and provide guidance made a significant impact on my development.

At that point, the environment changed from structured learning to something much closer to a real SOC workflow. It no longer felt like a guided lab—it felt like being handed a ticket and expected to work through it properly. The expectation was clear: approach the investigation with focus, accuracy, and efficiency.

While I had the ability to ask questions, the guidance I received was intentionally limited. Answers were often high-level, and I was encouraged to think through problems on my own. I was also reminded not to spend too much time on any one issue, reinforcing the importance of ticket handling time and prioritization in a real SOC environment.

With that in mind, I approached each incident with a more disciplined mindset—working methodically, validating findings, and making decisions with both accuracy and efficiency in mind. It was a shift from learning concepts to applying them under realistic expectations, and it marked a major turning point in how I approached incident response.
